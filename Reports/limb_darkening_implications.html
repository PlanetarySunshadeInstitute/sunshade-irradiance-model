Limb Darkening & Sunshade Placement

Topline implications of solar limb darkening for constellation design and shade effectiveness — drawing on validated model output and mathematical walkthrough.

01   Theory & Implementation

We understand how this is modeled — and we trust it

The PSF model implements limb darkening as a power series in cos θ (Eq. 1.4.1), using coefficients sourced from Tripathi et al. (2020) for total solar intensity. The profile is:

Limb darkening profile

I(θ) / I(0) = 0.61 + 0.39 · cos θ  —  intensity at disk center is 1.00; at the limb it falls to 0.61.

We traced the implementation end-to-end: from the Excel coefficients through import_Spectral_Data normalization (via Wallis integrals), through power_Series_Of_Matrix, and into both analysis_Irradiance and analysis_Shaded_Irradiance. The math is correctly implemented. A numerical comparison run — toggling C1/C2 between (0.61, 0.39) and (1, 0) — produced outputs consistent with theory.

02   The Effectiveness Crossover

Shades closer to the solar center punch above their weight

Because the solar disk is brighter at center than at the limb, where a shade falls on the disk directly determines how much flux it blocks. A shade intercepting sunlight near the center of the solar disk blocks more flux per unit area than simple geometry would predict. Near the edge, the reverse is true.

Inside the crossover radius (r/R < 0.83), limb darkening makes shades more effective than a uniform-disk model would predict. Outside it, shades are less effective. The crossover point sits at roughly θ ≈ 56° from disk center — about 83% of the way to the solar limb.

03   Constellation Design Implications

Proximity to the sun–Earth line is a force multiplier

For a sunshade constellation operating near the sun–Earth L1 point, shades aligned close to the sun–Earth line cast shadows that intersect the solar disk near its center — well within the crossover radius. In this regime, limb darkening works in our favor: each shade element blocks slightly more flux than a uniform-disk model would predict, making the constellation modestly more effective overall.

Design implication

Constellations concentrated near the sun–Earth line benefit from limb darkening. As shades are displaced off-axis — whether for orbital mechanics reasons or to target specific latitudes — their projected angle on the solar disk increases, eventually crossing the θ ≈ 56° threshold where limb darkening begins to reduce effectiveness.

This creates a meaningful tradeoff for spatially selective shading designs. Targeting specific latitudes on Earth requires off-axis shade placement, which shifts the shade's projection toward the solar limb. The gains in geographic selectivity must be weighed against the real reduction in flux-blocking efficiency. At the extreme, a shade near the solar limb (r/R > 0.83) blocks substantially less flux than the same shade near center — the falloff is steep, from ~63% of peak effectiveness at the crossover to just ~6% at the very limb.

In practice, the geometric cos θ foreshortening effect (Lambert's law) dominates over limb darkening itself in driving this falloff. Limb darkening adds a secondary but non-negligible correction on top — one that shifts the effective crossover slightly inward compared to a uniform-disk assumption.

Bottom line

Limb darkening modestly rewards on-axis placement and modestly penalizes off-axis placement. For most practical constellation geometries concentrated near the optical axis, the net effect is a slight boost in effectiveness (~5%). For selective-shading designs targeting off-axis positions, it is a real efficiency cost that should be explicitly modeled — not assumed away.
